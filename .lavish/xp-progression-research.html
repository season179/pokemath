PokeMath’s progression economy

The mechanism is broader than “leveling”: it is the rule that turns mathematical challenge into monster level, XP reward, anti-farming pressure, and a predictable pace.

Recommendation: Pokémon’s idea, Fire Emblem’s clarity, PokeMath’s own numbers.
Each Standard spans 30 levels—180 across the six-year journey. A same-level normal monster takes about 10 wins early, rising gradually to about 28 near level 180. Meadow alone represents roughly 345 fair battles, not five quick level-ups.

What established games already solved

Borrow the durable patterns, not their historical complexity.

Pokémon

borrow

Enemy species and level determine its base yield. In the scaled generations, the winner’s level relative to the defeated Pokémon also changes the award.

Use: enemy challenge + relative-level scaling.

Avoid copying the opaque 2.5-power formula or six species growth groups.

Fire Emblem

strong fit

XP explicitly follows enemy level minus player level. Many entries add boss bonuses, caps, and a minimum reward.

Use: a readable level-gap table, guardian multiplier, 1-XP floor.

This is easier to tune and explain than one large nonlinear equation.

RuneScape

counterexample

Its next-level requirement grows by roughly 10%; after the early game, required XP roughly doubles every seven levels.

Reject: an exponential grind for a child’s learning path.

PokeMath should measure practice, not endurance.

The proposed model

Three small rules. Each has one job, so future tuning does not create accidental side effects.

1 · place progression

Route → monster level

monsterLevel = (standard − 1) × 30 + routeTier

routeTier is 1–30. Each island’s landmarks and topic arcs distribute those tiers across a full school year.

2 · price the challenge

Monster + question → base XP

baseXP = 20 + 2 × monsterLevel

Then multiply by question rank: 80%, 90%, 100%, 120%, or 150%. Rank comes from TP, item format, reasoning steps, and calibrated answer data—not merely the operation symbol.

3 · compare fairly

Final kill reward

round(baseXP × question × gap × effort)

If the monster is 10+ levels below the player, override the result to exactly 1 XP. Apply this override last, including for guardians.

Keep the dimensions separate. Monster level represents where the encounter sits in the year-long journey. Question rank represents cognitive challenge. HP and effort compensate for battle workload. This separation makes all three independently tunable.

XP cannot guarantee “one calendar year.” A determined child can always binge finite content. The honest target is enough progression for a year of normal use: 8–12 battles per week makes Meadow last about 29–43 school weeks. Eight landmark/topic arcs, mini-guardians, collection, evolution, and mastery milestones must provide the long-term structure; inflating XP alone would only create grind.

Guardrails before implementation

Ship deterministic first

Do not make rewards depend on answer speed, time played, streaks, or an opaque adaptive model. The same encounter should pay the same XP.

Calibrate challenge separately

Log question rank, first-attempt correctness, hint use, and abandonment. Re-rank bad questions; do not keep changing the XP formula to compensate for mislabeled content.

Reward learning on gentle defeat

Keep kill XP tied to victory, but preserve mastery/attempt data for every correct answer. A child who loses after several correct answers did not learn “nothing.”

Tune with observed battles

First playtest target: 10 fair wins at level 1, 13 by the Meadow guardian, and 28 near level 180; 70–85% first-attempt correctness on on-level content; and no more than one-quarter level from one guardian.
