Make every battle move the journey forward

XP should feel visible, earned, and understandable—without turning every ordinary monster into a long results ceremony.

The recommendation: give the player a compact persistent XP bar, then reuse that same visual in a tap-to-continue victory drawer. Only promote to a centered overlay when the player actually levels up. The pet keeps HP; the player owns XP.

3 · When a level is crossed

This is the earned celebration. Dim the battle behind one focused card, show only real changes, and wait for the child to continue.

One reward sequence, paced by importance

Ordinary victories remain quick; meaningful transitions receive room to breathe.

1

A short 300–500ms victory beat. No long unskippable flourish.

2

+86 XP and money appear immediately.

3
Bar moves

Animate from the exact before value to the exact after value.

4
Explain why

Show bonus chips only for non-default modifiers.

5
Escalate if earned

No level crossed: continue. Level crossed: open the level-up card.

Input rule: the first tap during the count fast-forwards the animation; the next tap continues. Important results never disappear on a timer.

Recommended save migration

Preserve the player’s current highest creature level, then map the old fractional progress (oldXp / 20) into the new level threshold. After migration, creature XP and level stop driving permanent progression. A silent reset would be simpler but wrong once children have saves.

Accessibility is part of the mechanic

A progress display fails if a child cannot perceive or read it.

Never blue alone

Pair fill with 284 / 484 XP, a level label, and a distinct border. Color is reinforcement, not the only signal.

Respect reduced motion

Snap the count and bar to their final values. Keep the numbers and level-up card; remove scaling, panning, and confetti.

Let the reader decide

Text and rewards wait for player input. Optional count ticks and one level-up chime follow the game’s mute setting.

Research basis

Current guidance plus established game patterns; the mockups use PokeMath’s own visual language rather than copying another game.

Nielsen Norman Group · Visibility of system status Apple HIG · Progress indicators MDN · prefers-reduced-motion Game Accessibility Guidelines · Color is not enough Game Accessibility Guidelines · Player-paced text Duolingo · Small goals and finish lines Pokémon GO · Player-owned Trainer XP Interface In Game · Pokémon Let’s Go level-up result

The durable pattern across these references: show a truthful determinate state, reveal the result of the action quickly, make important text player-paced, and celebrate meaningful thresholds more strongly than routine increments.
